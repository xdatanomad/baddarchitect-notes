# LinkedIn Content Plan: The Demo Is Not the Product. The Workflow Is the Product.

- Source article: `content/articles/challenges/01_demo_workflow_product.md`
- Status: planned
- Recommended cadence: Six short posts over roughly three weeks, spaced 2-4 business days apart.

## Article Positioning

- Reader: AI architects, CTOs, product leaders, founders, and AI adoption leads trying to move impressive AI demos into deployed business workflows.
- Main argument: AI value does not come from a clever prompt or local prototype. It comes from a production workflow with triggers, context, permissions, memory, observability, governance, and measurable outcomes.
- Core takeaway: Before building another agent, map the workflow. If the team cannot explain the trigger, systems, context, human approval path, failure behavior, metrics, and owner, the demo is not ready to become a product.

## Planned Posts

### short-01 The Better Workbench Problem

- Type: short video script
- Angle: Acknowledge that demos can be genuinely useful, but they often improve one person's workbench instead of changing the business workflow.
- Hook: "Your AI demo may be useful and still not be a product."
- Takeaway: If the output still has to be copied, checked, routed, and entered somewhere else, the workflow has not been automated yet.
- Recommended timing: Day 1
- Generator guidance: Frame the post as practical and non-dismissive. Make clear that useful demos matter, but they are not the same as deployed workflows.

## Opening Post Alternatives

### alt-01 The Workflow Has Gravity

- Hook: "AI demos feel light. Business workflows have gravity."
- Use when: The generator wants a more memorable, metaphor-driven opening focused on triggers, permissions, systems of record, approvals, retries, audit trails, cost limits, and consequences.

### alt-02 Useful Is Not Deployed

- Hook: "A useful AI prototype is not the same thing as a deployed workflow."
- Use when: The generator wants a more executive/product-oriented opening focused on ownership, observability, governance, and measurable business value.

### short-02 The Demo Trap

- Type: short video script
- Angle: Show why local AI productivity often fails to become organizational leverage.
- Hook: "Most AI demos do not fail because the model is bad. They stall because nobody owns the workflow."
- Takeaway: Local productivity can be useful, but it is not transformation if the output still needs manual cleanup, copying, checking, routing, and re-entry.
- Recommended timing: Day 3 or Day 4
- Generator guidance: Avoid dismissing demos. Frame the demo trap as a normal early stage that becomes a problem when leadership mistakes scattered productivity for deployed capability.

### short-03 Workflow Questions Before Agent Questions

- Type: short video script
- Angle: Turn the workflow-readiness map into a practical leadership diagnostic.
- Hook: "Before asking which agent framework to use, ask what starts the workflow."
- Takeaway: The first design questions should be about triggers, systems touched, write paths, business metrics, ownership, and failure behavior.
- Recommended timing: Week 2, early week
- Generator guidance: Use a compact checklist feel, but do not list all twelve readiness-map questions. Pick four or five high-signal questions that fit a short post.

### short-04 Context Is Not Decoration

- Type: short video script
- Angle: Explain why generic AI output fails inside real operations.
- Hook: "Without context, AI can sound fluent and still be operationally useless."
- Takeaway: Production workflows need governed context from systems of record, customer history, policy, prior decisions, and recent events.
- Recommended timing: Week 2, late week
- Generator guidance: Use a sales, support, or customer-success example from the article. Make the point that context quality affects trust, not just output quality.

### short-05 RAG Is Not Operational Memory

- Type: short video script
- Angle: Separate retrieval from memory and stateful workflow design.
- Hook: "RAG can find information. It does not automatically know what changed."
- Takeaway: A workflow needs operational memory: what was approved, what failed, what changed, what the customer prefers, and what should happen next.
- Recommended timing: Week 3, early week
- Generator guidance: Keep this technical but accessible. Do not attack RAG. Position it as one useful component that does not replace memory, state, ownership, or orchestration.

### short-06 Make Agents Boring Enough to Operate

- Type: short video script
- Angle: Connect production AI agents to engineering discipline and operating requirements.
- Hook: "A serious AI agent needs more than a prompt and a model key."
- Takeaway: Production agents need secure tool access, identity, permissions, retries, observability, evals, cost monitoring, versioning, rollback, and human approval paths.
- Recommended timing: Week 3, late week
- Generator guidance: This should feel like an architect's reality check, not a tool roundup. Mention categories only; avoid turning the post into a vendor comparison.
